Trust issue with AI

June 27, 2026 3-minute read

Nowadays, writing code has become much “cheaper” in terms of time thanks to AI. However, this creates another major problem: project cognitive debt. When every line of code is written in minutes, the human brain seems unable to keep up and assess its correctness. Although this has happened in the past, resulting in bugs, with the rapid development of AI, the probability of us detecting and fixing bugs in time has decreased significantly.

And that leads to a bigger problem: mutual trust among the programming team. When AI makes programmers more reliant on AI and stop thinking deeply about solutions, instead they just use AI to find ready-made solutions that the AI has learned. It’s like a troop of monkeys finding a fire; instead of excitedly using the fire to improve their lives, the monkeys become frightened of what they didn’t understand.

When I use AI to design systems, it can give me very novel designs. Because I myself have never encountered similar problems when working with systems before, I feel like a monkey found a torch. And when that monkey brought the torch and showed it to the whole troop, instead of rejoicing at finding a new tool, the troop panicked and tried to get rid of it. The simple reason is that the troop had similar levels of understanding and had never known how to use fire before.

But everything has a reason. For example, if a monkey finds a sweet fruit that it and the rest of the troop have never seen before, it wants to share that sweet fruit with the rest of the troop, unaware that the fruit is poisonous. And the poison only takes effect some time after eating it. If the whole troop hastily accepts the fruit, it is very likely that no monkeys will survive.

Returning to the topic of AI, whether it is a terrifying but incredibly useful force for human civilization, or a sweet fruit laced with poison, we cannot yet tell. But we need a method to help the monkeys assess the benefits and harms it brings. I’m not offering a solution here, but merely stating the problem. Because for me, a quiet monkey in a troop, I just observe and wait for the others to do what they want. I may not be the first monkey to discover that fire can cook meat, but I also won’t die to a poisionous fruite.

We often see people changing lives and the world with new inventions and think we have to do the same. But the truth about the other monkeys that died because of curiosity is completely ignored. If you can overcome immediate curiosity and a desire to win, you will continue to survive. We weren’t born to succeed and become famous; we were born to survive and perpetuate the next generation.

Apes together strong.
