My Home Lab

April 2, 2026 3-minute read
Self Hosting
K8s • K3s • Cloudflare
Even though I have years of experience in developing web applications, when it comes to self-hosting my own stuff, it is still not as obvious to me.

I use a free GitHub Page to host my blog and map it to my custom domain. I don’t want to deal with a server because it introduces cost over time if I use a cloud server. But sometimes, I really want to have my own server, control all of my private data instead of handing it all over to Google. So while I was unemployed, I played with my home lab, which is just a modem + an old PC, to see what I could achieve.

At the beginning, I set it up as an Ubuntu server box. Any services were run on a podman rootless container and registered as systemd services, listening on localhost and their own high ports. I used nginx to proxy custom subdomains to each service. Certbot helped me automate my HTTPS configuration as well. That worked just fine, with no headache.

At this point, I thought I had mastered the technique to self-host my servers. And it only cost me an electricity bill.

But why stop there? I hadn’t used all its power yet, so I decided to over-engineer for fun and benefit.

So I wiped out the existing setup. Installed kubeadm, kubectl, …, and dove into the Kubernetes setup rabbit hole. It took me several attempts running kubeadm reset and kubeadm init to be able to run some services.

I spent my time converting compose.yml files to Deployment and Service objects and staring at the kubectl get pods output, waiting for the pods to come up, but they just wouldn’t.

I then figured out that I had to remove the taints on my cluster node to tell k8s to create pods on it (which is not recommended in production).

When I needed to mount a volume, I had no idea why my PVC was pending the whole time (later I learned kubeadm doesn’t include a default PV driver).

But the most painful part was configuring ingress with kubeadm. I just had no idea how it worked at all. I knew LoadBalancer in terms of the traditional definition. But what exactly is a LoadBalancer in k8s? I didn’t get it.

I kept my nginx proxy on and tried to forward k8s internal traffic to the host. But it didn’t work the way I imagined it would.

At this point, I knew kubeadm was too advanced for a noob like me. I ended up giving up on my kubeadm setup.

So I decided to switch completely to k3s, which in my opinion is a nice starter for k8s newbies. It is kid-friendly but more serious than minikube.

Adding ingress is now easier and smoother than before (I stopped my own nginx service and let k3s deal with all that; now I only need to specify the hostname in the ingress).

Even though it’s still messy, I think I’ve become more familiar with k8s than I was a few months ago. And I can even apply some of my experience to my daily job.
